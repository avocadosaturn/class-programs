Why do we divide d_m by k2 to get the transfer function?

This gets at the difference between what CLASS actually outputs and what you conventionally call "the transfer function."

What d_m actually is

get_transfer returns CLASS's density transfer functions, so d_m is the matter density contrast δm(k) (in synchronous gauge, per unit primordial curvature perturbation). It is not the conventionally-normalized transfer function T(k) that you want to plot.

Why there's a factor of k2

The density contrast and the gravitational potential are tied together by the Poisson equation:

−k2Φ(k)=4πGa2ρ¯δm(k)⟹δm(k)∝k2Φ(k)

The conventional transfer function T(k) describes the evolution of the potential (relative to the primordial spectrum), normalized so that T(k)→1 on large scales (k→0). On large (super-horizon) scales the potential is frozen — Φ is essentially constant and scale-independent — so through Poisson:

δm(k)∝k2T(k),T(k)→k→0const.

That means the raw d_m you get from CLASS grows like k2 even where nothing physically interesting is happening. If you plotted d_m directly, that k2 rise would dominate the figure and completely swamp the free-streaming suppression you're trying to see.

What dividing by k2 does

T(k)∝|δm(k)|k2

removes that trivial Poisson scaling, leaving the physical transfer function that is flat on large scales and shows the fall-off at small scales (high k) from WDM free streaming — exactly the shape the markdown cell interprets.

The subsequent line transfer1 /= transfer1[0] then normalizes it to 1 at the largest scale (smallest k), matching the T(k)→1 convention and letting you compare the three masses cleanly.
