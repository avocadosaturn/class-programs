Why do we divide d_m by $k^2$ to get the transfer function?

This gets at the difference between what CLASS actually outputs and what you conventionally call "the transfer function."

What d_m actually is

get_transfer returns CLASS's density transfer functions, so d_m is the matter density contrast $\delta_m(k)$ (in synchronous gauge, per unit primordial curvature perturbation). It is not the conventionally-normalized transfer function $T(k)$ that you want to plot.

Why there's a factor of $k^2$

The density contrast and the gravitational potential are tied together by the Poisson equation:

$$-k^2 \Phi(k) = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho\, \delta_m(k) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \delta_m(k) \propto k^2\, \Phi(k)$$

The conventional transfer function $T(k)$ describes the evolution of the potential (relative to the primordial spectrum), normalized so that $T(k)\to 1$ on large scales ($k\to 0$). On large (super-horizon) scales the potential is frozen — $\Phi$ is essentially constant and scale-independent — so through Poisson:

$$\delta_m(k) \propto k^2 \, T(k), \qquad T(k) \xrightarrow{k\to 0} \text{const.}$$

That means the raw d_m you get from CLASS grows like $k^2$ even where nothing physically interesting is happening. If you plotted d_m directly, that $k^2$ rise would dominate the figure and completely swamp the free-streaming suppression you're trying to see.

What dividing by $k^2$ does

$$T(k) \;\propto\; \frac{|\delta_m(k)|}{k^2}$$

removes that trivial Poisson scaling, leaving the physical transfer function that is flat on large scales and shows the fall-off at small scales (high $k$) from WDM free streaming — exactly the shape the markdown cell interprets.

The subsequent line transfer1 /= transfer1[0] then normalizes it to 1 at the largest scale (smallest $k$), matching the $T(k)\to 1$ convention and letting you compare the three masses cleanly.
