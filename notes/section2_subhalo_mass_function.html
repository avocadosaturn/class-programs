Section II — The Subhalo Mass Function

Tan, Dekker & Drlica-Wagner (2025), Mixed warm dark matter constraints using Milky Way satellite galaxy counts, PRD 111, 063079.

Vocabulary

  • Halo: dark matter clumps into gravitationally bound blobs called halos. A galaxy like the Milky Way sits inside a large dark matter halo.
  • Subhalo: big halos form by swallowing smaller ones, which survive as bound clumps orbiting inside the host halo. The Milky Way's halo contains thousands of these.
  • Why we care: the faint satellite galaxies orbiting the Milky Way live inside these subhalos. Counting subhalos predicts how many satellite galaxies there should be — the whole strategy of the paper.

The "mass function"

A mass function describes how many objects there are of each mass. It answers: "How many subhalos have a mass around $M$?" The paper writes it as:

$$\frac{dN_{\rm sub}}{dM}$$

Read this as: the number of subhalos ($dN_{\rm sub}$) per unit mass interval ($dM$) — a continuous histogram of subhalo masses.

  • Number of subhalos with mass between $M$ and $M+dM$ is $\dfrac{dN_{\rm sub}}{dM}\,dM$.
  • Total number in a mass range: integrate over that range.

Key fact: there are many more small subhalos than large ones — the mass function rises steeply toward low masses.

Why it's central to this paper

The dark matter physics shows up at the low-mass end:

  • Cold dark matter (CDM): structure forms down to tiny scales, so the mass function keeps rising toward small $M$ — lots of little subhalos.
  • Warm dark matter (WDM): fast-moving particles "free-stream" and erase small clumps, so the mass function is suppressed at low masses — fewer small subhalos.

Equation (1) states this cleanly:

$$\left(\frac{dN_{\rm sub}}{dM}\right)_{\rm MWDM} = \beta(M, \theta_{\rm WDM}, f_{\rm WDM}) \left(\frac{dN_{\rm sub}}{dM}\right)_{\rm CDM}$$

In words: take the CDM subhalo mass function and multiply it by a suppression function $\beta$ to get the warm-dark-matter version. Here $\beta$ is between 0 and 1: at high masses $\beta \approx 1$ (no change); at low masses $\beta$ drops below 1 (subhalos wiped out).

dark matter properties → shape of the subhalo mass function → number of subhalos → number of satellite galaxies → compare to DES/PS1 observations → constrain dark matter.
