Transfer function and the factor of k²: does the CAMB-shaped curve track δ or Φ?

This is exactly the crux, and there's no contradiction once you notice that the textbook's $T(k)$ is a ratio, whereas the raw CLASS output is not.

Your textbook's T(k) is a ratio, so the k² cancels

When you build $T(k)$ from $D(a)$, you're computing something like

$$T(k) \equiv \frac{\delta(k,a)}{\delta_{\rm ref}(k,a)}$$

where the reference $\delta_{\rm ref}$ is what that mode would have been if it had grown like the large-scale ($k < k_{\rm eq}$) modes the whole time — i.e. never suffered the Mészáros stall during radiation domination. That's precisely why:

  • $k < k_{\rm eq}$: mode enters the horizon during matter domination, grows $\propto D(a)$ the whole way, no suppression → $T = 1$ (flat).
  • $k > k_{\rm eq}$: mode enters during RD, freezes (log growth only) between $a_{\rm enter}$ and $a_{\rm eq}$, so it's down by $\sim (a_{\rm enter}/a_{\rm eq})^2 \propto (k_{\rm eq}/k)^2$ relative to the reference → $T \propto k^{-2}$.

So yes — you are genuinely tracking δ the whole way, and $T(k)$ is a real statement about δ's growth history (the Mészáros suppression).

Here's the key: both the numerator and the reference carry the same primordial-times-Poisson $k^2$ factor (they start from identical initial conditions). In a ratio, that $k^2$ cancels. Since $\delta \propto k^2\Phi$ at fixed $a$,

$$T(k) = \frac{\delta(k,a)}{\delta_{\rm ref}(k,a)} = \frac{k^2\Phi(k,a)}{k^2\Phi_{\rm ref}(k,a)} = \frac{\Phi(k,a)}{\Phi_{\rm ref}(k,a)}.$$

So your $T(k)$ is simultaneously the density-suppression ratio and the normalized potential transfer. Same number. That's why some textbooks (e.g. Dodelson) define $T(k)$ straight from $\Phi$ and get the identical flat-then-$k^{-2}$ curve. Neither framing is wrong.

Why CLASS is different — that's where the loose k² lives

The raw CLASS mTk output is not a ratio to an un-suppressed reference. It's

$$T^{\rm CLASS}_\delta(k) = \frac{\delta(k,a)}{\mathcal{R}(k)}$$

— density relative to the primordial curvature $\mathcal{R}$. Nothing cancels the Poisson $k^2$ here, so this quantity rises as $k^2$ at small $k$ (not flat). It does not look like your textbook curve.

To recover your textbook's CAMB-shaped $T(k)$, you divide the CLASS δ-output by $k^2$ (and renormalize). And that operation — $\delta/k^2$ — is exactly the map from the density to the potential (Poisson). That's the sense in which "dividing out $k^2$ gives you $\Phi$."

Putting it together

small kdescribes
your textbook $T(k)$ (a ratio) → 1 (flat) δ's growth suppression — numerically = Φ ratio
CLASS raw $\delta/\mathcal{R}$ $\propto k^2$ matter density relative to primordial $\mathcal{R}$
CLASS $\delta/\mathcal{R} \div k^2$ → const (flat) $\Phi/\mathcal{R}$ = your textbook $T(k)$

So your intuition ("we track δ") is correct, and the earlier statement ("the flat curve is proportional to $\Phi$") is correct — because in the ratio that defines $T(k)$, the $k^2$ common to δ and its reference cancels, leaving the same scale dependence as the potential. The only place a $k^2$ needs to be actively divided out by hand is when you start from CLASS's raw $\delta/\mathcal{R}$ output, which never formed that ratio for you.
