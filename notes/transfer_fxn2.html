Transfer function and the factor of k²: does the CAMB-shaped curve track δ or Φ?

This is exactly the crux, and there's no contradiction once you notice that the textbook's T(k) is a ratio, whereas the raw CLASS output is not.

Your textbook's T(k) is a ratio, so the k² cancels

When you build T(k) from D(a), you're computing something like

T(k)≡δ(k,a)δref(k,a)

where the reference δref is what that mode would have been if it had grown like the large-scale (k<keq) modes the whole time — i.e. never suffered the Mészáros stall during radiation domination. That's precisely why:

  • k<keq: mode enters the horizon during matter domination, grows ∝D(a) the whole way, no suppression → T=1 (flat).
  • k>keq: mode enters during RD, freezes (log growth only) between aenter and aeq, so it's down by ∼(aenter/aeq)2∝(keq/k)2 relative to the reference → T∝k−2.

So yes — you are genuinely tracking δ the whole way, and T(k) is a real statement about δ's growth history (the Mészáros suppression).

Here's the key: both the numerator and the reference carry the same primordial-times-Poisson k2 factor (they start from identical initial conditions). In a ratio, that k2 cancels. Since δ∝k2Φ at fixed a,

T(k)=δ(k,a)δref(k,a)=k2Φ(k,a)k2Φref(k,a)=Φ(k,a)Φref(k,a).

So your T(k) is simultaneously the density-suppression ratio and the normalized potential transfer. Same number. That's why some textbooks (e.g. Dodelson) define T(k) straight from Φ and get the identical flat-then-k−2 curve. Neither framing is wrong.

Why CLASS is different — that's where the loose k² lives

The raw CLASS mTk output is not a ratio to an un-suppressed reference. It's

TδCLASS(k)=δ(k,a)R(k)

— density relative to the primordial curvature R. Nothing cancels the Poisson k2 here, so this quantity rises as k2 at small k (not flat). It does not look like your textbook curve.

To recover your textbook's CAMB-shaped T(k), you divide the CLASS δ-output by k2 (and renormalize). And that operation — δ/k2 — is exactly the map from the density to the potential (Poisson). That's the sense in which "dividing out k2 gives you Φ."

Putting it together

small kdescribes
your textbook T(k) (a ratio) → 1 (flat) δ's growth suppression — numerically = Φ ratio
CLASS raw δ/R ∝k2 matter density relative to primordial R
CLASS δ/R÷k2 → const (flat) Φ/R = your textbook T(k)

So your intuition ("we track δ") is correct, and the earlier statement ("the flat curve is proportional to Φ") is correct — because in the ratio that defines T(k), the k2 common to δ and its reference cancels, leaving the same scale dependence as the potential. The only place a k2 needs to be actively divided out by hand is when you start from CLASS's raw δ/R output, which never formed that ratio for you.
