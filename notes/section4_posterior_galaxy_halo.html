Section IV — The "Posterior Distribution of the Galaxy–Halo Connection Parameters"

Tan, Dekker & Drlica-Wagner (2025), PRD 111, 063079. The model is from Nadler et al. (2020).

The phrase has three parts worth unpacking separately: (1) the galaxy–halo connection model, (2) its parameters, and (3) what a posterior distribution over those parameters is. Let's take them in order.

1. What the galaxy–halo connection model does

Section III gave us a population of dark matter subhalos. But a subhalo is invisible — we only see the galaxy that may live inside it. The galaxy–halo connection model is the recipe that "paints" a galaxy onto each subhalo: given a subhalo, does it host a galaxy, how bright is it, how big is it, and would a survey actually detect it?

Concretely, the predicted number of observable satellites is (Eq. 7):

$$n_{\rm sat} = \sum_i f_{{\rm gal},i}\,\times\,(1 - p_{{\rm disrupt},i})\,\times\,p_{{\rm detect},i}\,\times\,\beta_i$$

where $i$ runs over subhalos, $f_{\rm gal}$ is the probability the subhalo hosts a galaxy, $p_{\rm disrupt}$ the probability it was destroyed by baryonic effects, $p_{\rm detect}$ the survey detection probability, and $\beta$ the WDM suppression from Section II.

2. The parameters $\theta_{\rm model}$

Each ingredient above is not known exactly — it is controlled by a handful of tunable numbers. The paper writes the full set as

$$\theta_{\rm model} = \{\alpha,\ \sigma_M,\ \mathcal{A},\ n,\ \sigma_{\log R},\ M_{50},\ \sigma_{\rm gal},\ B\}.$$

Roughly, these parametrize four physical relations (details in Nadler et al. 2020):

RelationParametersWhat they control
Luminosity ↔ halo$\alpha,\ \sigma_M$faint-end slope of the satellite luminosity function, and the scatter around the mean brightness–halo relation
Galaxy size ↔ halo$\mathcal{A},\ n,\ \sigma_{\log R}$amplitude and slope of the galaxy-size relation, plus its scatter
Occupation fraction$M_{50},\ \sigma_{\rm gal}$halo mass at which 50% of halos host a galaxy, and how sharp that cutoff is ($f_{\rm gal}$)
Baryonic disruption$B$strength of subhalo disruption by the galactic disk ($p_{\rm disrupt}$)

The exact definition of each symbol is set in Nadler et al. (2020); the grouping above is the physical intuition, not verbatim definitions. If you want, I can pull the precise definitions from that reference.

3. What a "posterior distribution" is

This is the Bayesian-statistics core. We have model parameters $\theta$ and data $D$ (the observed satellite counts). We want to know: given what we observed, which parameter values are plausible? That is captured by the posterior distribution $P(\theta \mid D)$ — a probability distribution over parameter space. Bayes' theorem builds it:

$$\underbrace{P(\theta \mid D)}_{\text{posterior}} \;=\; \frac{\overbrace{P(D \mid \theta)}^{\text{likelihood}}\;\overbrace{P(\theta)}^{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{P(D)}_{\text{evidence}}} \;\propto\; P(D \mid \theta)\,P(\theta)$$
  • Prior $P(\theta)$: what we believed about the parameters before looking at the data (e.g. allowed ranges).
  • Likelihood $P(D \mid \theta)$: how probable the observed counts are if the parameters were $\theta$. Here the paper bins satellites by absolute magnitude and surface brightness and assumes the count in each bin is Poisson: $$P(D\mid\theta) = \prod_{\rm bins} \frac{\lambda_b^{\,k_b}\,e^{-\lambda_b}}{k_b!},$$ where $\lambda_b$ is the predicted count in bin $b$ (from $n_{\rm sat}$) and $k_b$ the observed count.
  • Posterior $P(\theta \mid D)$: the updated belief after folding in the data — the answer we actually want.

So the "posterior distribution of the galaxy–halo connection parameters" is simply: the probability distribution over the 8 parameters $\theta_{\rm model}$ (plus $f_{\rm WDM}$) that is consistent with the observed DES and PS1 satellite counts.

4. "Sample the posterior" with EMCEE

The posterior lives in a ~9-dimensional space and has no closed form, so you cannot just write it down. Instead you sample it: draw many parameter sets $\theta$ in proportion to their posterior probability, using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler — here emcee. The cloud of samples is the posterior: dense where parameters are likely, sparse where unlikely.

In Section IV the procedure is:

Fix a WDM mass $m_{\rm WDM}$ → let the WDM fraction $f_{\rm WDM}$ (and the 8 model parameters) vary → run emcee to sample the posterior → the resulting distribution over $f_{\rm WDM}$ tells you which fractions are allowed by the data, giving the constraint.
